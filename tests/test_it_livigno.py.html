* The avalanche-prone locations are described in greater detail in the avalanche bulletin (altitude, slope aspect, type of terrain).
• moderately steep terrain: slopes shallower than about 30 degrees
• steep slopes: slopes steeper than about 30 degrees
• very steep, extreme terrain: particularly adverse terrain related to slope angle (more than about 40 degrees), terrain profile, proximity to ridge, smoothness of underlying ground surface

** Additional loads:
• low: individual skier / snowboarder, riding softly, not falling; snowshoer; group with good spacing (minimum 10m) keeping distances
• high: two or more skiers / snowboarders etc. without good spacing (or without intervals); snowmachine; explosives

DANGER DESCRIPTION
Above the treeline within the snowpack persistent weak layers are recorded and it’s likely to trigger slab avalanches that can reach medium sizes. In addition mainly on the northern slopes, with the winds and snowfall, medium sized unstable wind slabs formed their release may also affect deeper layers and avalanches may increase their size. Spontaneous sluff from southern slope is probable.
TRAVEL AND TERRAIN ADVICE
Shooting cracks and Whumpfing sounds are clear danger signs, approach steep slopes and transitions from deep to shallow snowpack with caution; unstable areas are difficult to detect. Pay attention to wind-loaded areas and slope changes. New snow makes it difficult to avoid rocks and it is likely to hit them either by skiing, falling or being caught in an avalanche. Conditions are mostly unfavourable for skiing excursions.
SNOW COVER CHARACTERISTICS
Above the treeline the snowpack has a weak structure with persistent weak layers alternating with melt-freeze crusts which are covered by a more cohesive slab. The snowpack-surface shows wind accumulations covered by a layer of dry loose snow, while in the more wind-sheltered areas 20-40 cm of new snow are recorded. Below the treeline the snowpack is thin.
